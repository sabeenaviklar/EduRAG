# PHYSICS - Newton's Laws of Motion

## Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia)
An object at rest stays at rest and an object in motion stays in motion 
with the same speed and in the same direction unless acted upon by an 
unbalanced force. This is also called the Law of Inertia.

Example: A book on a table will not move unless someone pushes it. 
A moving car will continue moving if the engine is turned off (though 
friction will eventually stop it).

## Newton's Second Law (F = ma)
The acceleration of an object depends on the net force acting on it 
and the mass of the object. Mathematically: F = m × a
Where:
  - F = Force (measured in Newtons, N)
  - m = Mass (measured in kilograms, kg)
  - a = Acceleration (measured in m/s²)

Example: Pushing a shopping cart — the harder you push (more force), 
the faster it accelerates. A heavier cart requires more force for the 
same acceleration.

## Newton's Third Law (Action-Reaction)
For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.
When object A exerts a force on object B, object B exerts an equal 
and opposite force on object A.

Example: When you jump, you push down on the ground (action), 
and the ground pushes you up (reaction), launching you into the air.

# BIOLOGY - Photosynthesis

## What is Photosynthesis?
Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants, algae, and some 
bacteria convert light energy (usually from the sun) into chemical 
energy stored as glucose (sugar).

## The Equation
6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
Carbon dioxide + Water + Light → Glucose + Oxygen

## Two Stages of Photosynthesis

### 1. Light-Dependent Reactions (in the thylakoid)
- Chlorophyll absorbs sunlight
- Water molecules are split (photolysis), releasing oxygen as a byproduct
- ATP and NADPH are produced (energy carriers)

### 2. Light-Independent Reactions / Calvin Cycle (in the stroma)
- CO₂ from the air is "fixed" (attached to organic molecules)
- ATP and NADPH from Stage 1 are used to produce glucose
- The cycle regenerates the molecule that captures CO₂

## Importance of Photosynthesis
1. Produces oxygen that most living organisms breathe
2. Forms the base of the food chain (primary producers)
3. Removes CO₂ from the atmosphere, helping regulate climate

# HISTORY - World War I (1914-1918)

## Main Causes (MAIN Acronym)
- **M**ilitarism: European powers built up massive armies and navies
- **A**lliances: Europe was divided into two armed camps (Triple Alliance vs Triple Entente)
- **I**mperialism: Competition for colonies created international tensions
- **N**ationalism: Strong national pride, especially in the Balkans

## The Spark
The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary 
on June 28, 1914, in Sarajevo by Gavrilo Princip (a Bosnian Serb nationalist)
triggered the alliance system and led to a declaration of war.

## Key Events
- 1914: War begins; Germany invades Belgium; trench warfare begins
- 1915: Gallipoli Campaign; Italy joins the Allies
- 1916: Battle of the Somme (1 million casualties); Battle of Verdun
- 1917: USA enters the war; Russian Revolution
- 1918: Germany's allies collapse; Armistice signed on November 11

## Consequences
- ~17 million deaths (military and civilian)
- Collapse of four empires: Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, German, Russian
- Treaty of Versailles (1919): harsh terms placed on Germany, leading to WWII

# MATHEMATICS - The Pythagorean Theorem

## Statement
In a right-angled triangle, the square of the hypotenuse (the side 
opposite the right angle) is equal to the sum of the squares of the 
other two sides.

## Formula
a² + b² = c²
Where c is the hypotenuse and a, b are the other two sides.

## Example
If a = 3 and b = 4:
c² = 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25
c = √25 = 5

This is the famous "3-4-5 right triangle."

## Applications
1. Construction: Ensuring walls and floors are perfectly perpendicular
2. Navigation: Calculating distances between two points
3. Computer graphics: Calculating distances in 2D and 3D space

# ECONOMICS - Supply and Demand

## The Law of Demand
As the price of a good increases, the quantity demanded decreases 
(assuming all other factors are held constant). This gives the demand 
curve a downward slope.

## The Law of Supply
As the price of a good increases, the quantity supplied increases 
(producers want to sell more at higher prices). This gives the supply 
curve an upward slope.

## Market Equilibrium
The equilibrium price (or "market-clearing price") is where the 
quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied. At this point, 
there is no surplus or shortage.

## Shifts in the Curves
- Demand shifts right: increased income, tastes, population, or 
  expectations of higher prices
- Supply shifts right: improved technology, lower input costs, 
  more producers

## Real-World Example
During COVID-19 mask shortages:
- Demand for masks surged (shift right) → prices rose
- Over time, supply increased as more manufacturers entered the market
- Eventually, a new equilibrium was reached at a lower price
